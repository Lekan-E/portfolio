Ottawa: Auto Theft Analysis 2018 – 2023

Analysing over 7,000 vehicle thefts recorded by the Ottawa Police Service to uncover theft patterns, recovery rates, financial losses, and the city's highest-risk zones.

The Problem

Auto theft in Canada reached a crisis point between 2018 and 2023, with Ottawa recording thousands of vehicle thefts annually. Beyond the personal cost to victims, the aggregate financial and social impact on the city demands a clear, data-driven understanding of where, when, and how these thefts occur.

This project analyses the complete Ottawa Police Service auto theft dataset for the six-year period — quantifying trends, mapping risk zones, and providing stakeholders with evidence to improve prevention and recovery outcomes.

Key Findings

  • Year-on-Year Surge: Auto theft incidents increased sharply from 2021 onward, with 2022 and 2023 recording the highest volumes in the six-year period — reflecting a national trend driven by organised vehicle export rings targeting keyless-entry vehicles.
  • Low Recovery Rates: A significant proportion of stolen vehicles were never recovered, pointing to the organised, export-driven nature of many thefts where vehicles are moved across borders rapidly after theft.
  • High-Risk Geographies: Theft incidents were concentrated in specific Ottawa neighbourhoods and commercial corridors, with certain postal code areas accounting for a disproportionate share of total incidents.
  • Financial Impact: Estimated financial losses across the six-year period ran into tens of millions of dollars when accounting for vehicle values and insurance costs — a significant economic burden on the city.
  • Time Patterns: Overnight hours (10pm–4am) accounted for the majority of incidents, with weekends showing elevated theft rates consistent with reduced street activity and surveillance.

Methodology

01

SQL Data Processing

Raw incident data was imported and queried in SQL to aggregate theft counts by year, month, neighbourhood, and recovery status. YoY change rates were calculated using window functions.

02

Python Analysis

Python (Pandas, Matplotlib) was used for deeper trend analysis, financial loss estimation, and time-of-day breakdowns. Geocoding was applied to convert address data into mappable coordinates.

03

Geospatial Mapping

Incident locations were plotted on an Ottawa ward map to identify theft hotspots. Density maps highlighted the highest-risk zones for targeted policing and resident awareness.
